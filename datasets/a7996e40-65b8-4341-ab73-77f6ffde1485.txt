This process takes place within the plant's chloroplasts and includes two main stages: the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle).

Oxygen is released as a waste product through the splitting of water molecules.

The ozone layer, located in the Earth's stratosphere, plays a critical role in protecting life on our planet. The primary function of the ozone layer is to absorb and filter harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation emitted by the sun. UV radiation is divided into three types: UVA, UVB, and UVC. While UVA and UVB can pass through the atmosphere and have various effects on life, UVC is the most harmful type.

The ozone layer effectively absorbs most of the sun's UVC radiation and a significant portion of UVB radiation, preventing them from reaching the Earth's surface. This protection is vital for maintaining the delicate balance of life on Earth, as excessive UV radiation can cause various damaging effects, including:

1. Skin cancer: Increased exposure to UV radiation can lead to a higher risk of skin cancer in humans and animals.
2. Cataracts and other eye problems: Prolonged exposure to UV radiation can cause cataracts – the clouding of the lens in the eyes – and other eye-related issues.
3. Harm to marine ecosystems: Increased UV radiation can penetrate the upper layer of the ocean, impacting delicate aquatic life and disrupting the food chain.
4. Crop damage: High levels of UV radiation can harm vital food crops, resulting in reduced yields and threatening global food security.

To sum up, the ozone layer is essential for protecting life on Earth by absorbing harmful UV radiation from the sun, thus safeguarding human health, preserving ecosystems, and ensuring stable crop production.

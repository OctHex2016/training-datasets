Photosynthesis is a biological process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy. It occurs in the chloroplasts of plant cells, where the light-absorbing pigment chlorophyll is present. The process involves two main stages: the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle). In the light-dependent reactions, sunlight is absorbed by chlorophyll, resulting in the production of ATP (adenosine triphosphate) and NADPH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate), which transport energy to the Calvin cycle. Water molecules are also split, releasing oxygen as a byproduct. In the light-independent reactions, atmospheric carbon dioxide is fixed into an organic molecule through a series of enzyme-catalyzed reactions, ultimately leading to the production of glucose and other sugars. These sugars can be utilized by the plant for growth, energy, and reproduction or stored for future use.
